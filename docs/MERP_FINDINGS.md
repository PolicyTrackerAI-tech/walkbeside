## Arkansas

- **Scope:** `probate-only`
- **Citation:** Ark. Code Ann. § 20-76-436; Ark. Admin. Code 016.20.96-005 (Procedures Regarding the Recovery of Medical Payments from the Estates of Deceased Individuals); DHS PUB 428 (Your Guide to Medicaid Estate Recovery in Arkansas)
- **What can be claimed (as shipped):** DHS files its claim against the probate estate (including small estates administered by affidavit); the agency's own guide confirms that assets passing directly to a beneficiary outside probate — insurance proceeds, IRAs, pension plans, mutual funds, deferred compensation — are not collected, and that out-of-state property can be part of the estate. Arkansas does NOT use pre-death liens: per the DHS guide, 'Medicaid does not take nor put a lien against a recipient's home while he or she is alive'; if no probate is opened, DHS files a demand notice with the county clerk so it is notified if the house is sold or probated.
- **Notice/response window (as shipped):** Codified in the DHS rule: the estate representative may apply for a hardship waiver at the time notice of the estate is given to DHS or within 30 days after receiving DHS's notice of intent to recover (form DHS-20); the DHS committee decides within 30 days; an adverse decision may be appealed to the DHS Office of Appeals and Hearings no later than 30 days from the date of the notice of negative action.
- **Hardship (as shipped):** Hardship criteria are codified in the statute and DHS rule: waiver where the asset is the survivors'/beneficiaries' sole income-producing asset (statute) or — per the DHS rule — their sole source of income and that income is insufficient to meet living expenses and also repay the debt to DHS; where denying the inheritance would make a beneficiary newly eligible for public benefits (or receiving it would let them leave benefits); where the home is worth fifty percent (50%) or less of the average price of homes in the county; or for other compelling circumstances. DHS also waives claims that are not cost-effective. Application is due at the time notice of the estate is given to DHS or within 30 days of the DHS-20 notice of intent to recover.

## Delaware

- **Scope:** `probate-only`
- **Citation:** 25 Del. C. §§ 5001–5006 (Liens and Estate Recoveries); Delaware Social Services Manual (16 Del. Admin. Code 20000) § 20500 et seq. (lien trigger at DSSM 20500.6.1; hardship residency requirement at DSSM 20500.7.1, hardship financial conditions at DSSM 20500.7.2)
- **What can be claimed (as shipped):** Delaware's statutory recovery definition tracks the probate estate — "'Estate' means all real property, as well as all personal property, which constitutes assets of the individual's estate as described in Chapter 19 of Title 12" (25 Del. C. § 5001(c)) — and does not reach joint tenancy, trusts, or TOD assets. But Delaware layers on a pre-death lien program: DHSS places liens on the real property of recipients 55+ approved for long-term-care services once a facility stay reaches 60 days (25 Del. C. § 5002; DSSM 20500.6.1) — EXCEPT no lien is filed while a spouse, a child under 21 or blind/disabled child, or a qualifying sibling lawfully resides in the home. The lien dissolves if the recipient is discharged and returns home, and recovery may be taken "from the individual's estate or upon sale of property subject to a lien" — so a recorded lien can reach the home's sale proceeds even outside estate administration.
- **Notice/response window (as shipped):** No codified window in days. 25 Del. C. § 5002(a) requires notice and an opportunity for a hearing before the Department prior to imposing a lien (to contest whether the person can reasonably be expected to return home). DSSM 20500.2 requires written notice of the recovery/lien policy at application; recipients who turn 55 after their case is active get notice at their first financial redetermination after their 55th birthday. Neither the statute nor the DSSM codifies a deadline in days to respond to a recovery notice or to apply for an undue-hardship waiver.
- **Hardship (as shipped):** DSSM 20500.7.1–.7.2: undue hardship is available only to specified relatives — children, grandchildren, parents, or siblings of the recipient (current DSSM also lists a civil union partner), NOT any heir — who lived in the home continuously for at least 24 months immediately before the recipient's admission to long-term care (residency requirement at 20500.7.1), AND either receive federal/state assistance for living expenses (e.g., SSI, VA Aid and Attendance) with no other home to return to, OR have total family income at or below 200% of the federal poverty limit and convertible family resources of $3,000 or less (financial conditions at 20500.7.2). The lien is still filed but a moratorium is placed on it — DHSS will not force sale; under 25 Del. C. § 5005 the waiver "shall remain in effect only as long as the undue hardship condition continues" (a moratorium, not forgiveness). Separately, 25 Del. C. § 5003 exempts recovery while survivors include a spouse residing in the home, a blind/disabled child, a minor child (until majority), or a child or sibling who lived in the home 2+ years pre-admission and provided care that kept the recipient at home.
