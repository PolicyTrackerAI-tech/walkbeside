# Honest Funeral

**The only player in death-care paid by no one with a stake in the funeral price.**

Honest Funeral is a free, neutral funeral-price advocate for grieving families — wrapped in a light "what to do next" navigation layer — paid for by the institutions that serve the dying but have no stake in the funeral bill.

**The empty seat no one took.** Every party that "helps" a grieving family is conflicted. The funeral home sells the casket. The insurer wants the claim closed fast. The "end-of-life concierge" is paid by the insurer or employer and has zero incentive to push the funeral bill *down*. There is exactly one seat at the table that no funeral money has ever bought — the family's side of the price negotiation — and it is empty. We take it.

## The wedge

A **neutral funeral-PRICE advocate**: is this $9,000 quote fair for your metro? Here's the benchmark, here's the negotiation letter, here's the checklist for the next 30 days. **Free to families, forever** — families are never the payer. We monetize the institutions with no price stake. We do **not** wade into the estate/probate/grief swamp (that's a crowded, conflicted lane); we own the one thing nobody neutral covers: **what funerals actually cost, and whether you're being overcharged.**

## Why now

- **Regulatory tailwind.** The FTC is circling funeral price transparency; only ~18% of homes post prices online today. A neutral, citable price index is the thing regulators, journalists, and families reach for.
- **PE consolidation** is rolling up funeral homes and quietly raising prices — sharpening the need for a family-side advocate.
- **AI search rewards a citable neutral dataset.** "How much does a funeral cost in Phoenix?" increasingly resolves to whoever owns the defensible, neutral answer. We're building to *be* that answer.
- **A real, unfunded mandate.** ~5,800 Medicare-certified hospices are *required* to provide bereavement support (42 CFR 418.64) but get **no separate reimbursement** for it. We hand them a turnkey, compliant family benefit that fills a hole they already have to fill.

## The model, in one breath

Free to families → **hospices pay first, then employers** (B2B2C) → **funeral homes and insurers never pay us, ever** → the proprietary neutral price-and-outcomes dataset plus conflict-free trust is the moat → strategic acquisition in a category investors have already proven (Empathy: ~$162M raised, 50M+ covered lives).

**Why the scale engine is employers, with the math.** Hospices are the credible *first* customer, but the arithmetic is hard solo: at an illustrative ~$5K ACV, it takes ~200 hospice logos to reach $1M ARR. Employers invert that. A focused bereavement benefit prices in the EAP range (~$3.50 PEPM, anchored to published EAP market data); at a 1,000-employee company that's ~$42K ACV — so **~25 employer logos clear $1M ARR, and ~50 at a conservative $21K ACV.** Roughly a 4–8x lower logo count than hospice for the same revenue, at far better unit economics. *(Both the hospice $5K and the employer ACVs are illustrative arithmetic on published EAP/software benchmarks, not booked deals — but the 4–8x logo-count advantage holds across the plausible range.)*

**The employer wedge is the same wedge — and Empathy structurally can't copy it.** Empathy owns the employer/insurer bereavement channel, but it is *insurer- and employer-PAID*, which means it has no revenue incentive — and a real disincentive — to drive the funeral bill down. We sell employers the one component they can't get from Empathy or any insurer-paid benefit: a **neutral, family-side funeral-price advocate**, packaged as the differentiated piece of a bereavement benefit — the component that actually lowers what employees pay. That's a structural moat, not a feature gap they can ship around.

**Why they renew.** Once the tool sits inside a hospice's intake workflow and the aggregate proof report lands in their compliance/benefits file, ripping it out is costly — the embedding and switching cost is the renewal thesis, not goodwill.

## The honest part

**Institutional willingness-to-pay is unproven** — no paid customer yet — and the plan de-risks it cheaply: small paid hospice pilots before any scale spend. That's the one caveat that's genuinely load-bearing; everything else is earned.

The free-layer flywheel (reach millions of families → outcomes data → moat) is real upside but **unproven at its foundation: analytics isn't instrumented yet.** The base case doesn't need it. The cheap, immediate next step — instrument analytics — turns it from a hole into a plan, and the upside into something we can actually measure.

## Traction

The free product is **largely built at near-zero cost**: metro price tools, the "is this quote fair?" analyzer, 87 metro pages, a 63-term glossary, the family advocacy flow, and outcomes instrumentation pending merge. **Pre-revenue and pre-first-pilot by design** — we built the asset before spending a dollar on go-to-market.

Insurers remain a potential acquirer, never a payer: we chose the clean headline, **no insurer money, ever.**
