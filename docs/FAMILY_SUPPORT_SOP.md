# Family Support SOP — How we support a grieving family when they need a human

> **Status:** Live runbook. Owned by the founder. Last reviewed 2026-06-24.
> Aligns to [`OPERATING_PLAN.md`](OPERATING_PLAN.md) (the bible) and the six
> guardrails in [`../CLAUDE.md`](../CLAUDE.md). When this document and a guardrail
> seem to conflict, the guardrail wins — flag it and we fix this doc.

This is the operating manual for the moment a family stops using the tools and
needs a person. It is the human edge of the product: it is where trust is won or
lost, and where the proprietary outcomes data is born. Read Part 1 (boundaries)
before you ever answer a family.

---

## 0. The one-line standard

> A family in front of us at the worst moment of their life should feel that
> someone is **on their side, paid by no one with a stake in the price, and not
> in a hurry.** Calm, plain, useful. We give **information and advocacy** — never
> legal, financial, or medical advice — and we **never steer to a specific home.**

---

## 1. Hard boundaries (read first, no exceptions)

These are not style notes. They are the guardrails (Part 14 of the bible) as they
show up in a live conversation. Breaking one is the unrecoverable mistake.

| We DO | We do NOT |
|---|---|
| Explain options, prices, rights, and process in plain language | Tell a family which funeral home to use, or name "the one we'd pick" |
| Tell a family their FTC right to an itemized General Price List (GPL) | Give legal advice ("you should sue," "that's illegal, here's your case") |
| Flag a quote that looks high vs. local benchmarks, with the data | Give financial advice (investments, what to liquidate, tax moves) |
| Point to grief, hospice, VA, and benefits resources | Give medical or mental-health treatment advice |
| Take the family's side in a dispute with a home | Take a dollar from any funeral home or insurer, ever |
| Stay free to the family, always | Charge the grieving family, or upsell them anything |

**Three lines you can always fall back on, verbatim:**

- **On steering:** *"I can't tell you which home to choose — that's the one thing
  I won't do, because we take no money from any funeral home, which is exactly
  why you can trust what I show you. I can lay the options side by side so you can
  decide."*
- **On advice we can't give:** *"I'm not able to give legal/financial/medical
  advice, and I don't want to steer you wrong on something that serious. Here's
  what I can do, and here's who's the right person for that part."*
- **On who pays us:** *"You never pay us. We're paid by institutions like
  hospices, never by funeral homes or insurers. That's the whole point — no one
  who profits from the price is paying us."*

**When in doubt, slow down and route up.** Founder is the escalation path (Part
6). It is always correct to say *"Let me make sure I get this exactly right and
come back to you"* rather than guess on something load-bearing.

## 3. The intake reflex (first 60 seconds of any contact)

Run this quietly in every conversation before solving anything:

1. **Acknowledge, don't perform.** One plain line. ("I'm glad you reached out."
   Not "I am so deeply sorry for your devastating loss.")
2. **Triage severity** (P0/P1/P2 above) — is anything time-bound or distressing?
3. **Locate the case.** Are they an existing negotiation? Get enough to find them
   (email used, name) — but **collect the minimum.** No health details, no cause
   of death, nothing we don't need. (HIPAA minimization; the family
   self-enrolls.)
4. **Set one next step + a timeframe** before you end the contact. A family should
   never hang up or close the email without knowing what happens next and when.

## 4. Common scenarios + response scripts

Scripts are starting points — keep the substance, match the person. Plain,
unhurried, no performed empathy.

### 4.1 "A home is pressuring me / being pushy."
*Severity: usually P0.* The family feels cornered — our job is to put the floor
back under them.

> "You don't have to decide anything today, and you don't have to do it on their
> timeline. By law they have to give you an itemized price list and they can't
> require you to buy a package to get one thing. Let's look at exactly what
> they've quoted you. I'll stay on your side of this — and if it helps, I can
> tell you the specific lines that are over what's normal around you."

Then: surface the GPL right, compare against benchmarks, and (if they want)
help them get competing itemized quotes. **Do not** name a "better" home — lay
options side by side.

### 4.2 "Is this quote fair?"
*Severity: P1.* This is the core hook. Anchor only on numbers we can defend.

> "Send me the itemized list — a photo of the price sheet is perfect. I'll line
> it up against what families around you are typically quoted and flag anything
> that's high. I'll show you where the number comes from, not just a verdict."

Rules: only cite a benchmark we can stand behind (guardrail #4 — defensible
numbers, methodology exists). If we don't have enough local data, say so plainly:
*"I don't have a solid local benchmark for that one line yet, so I won't pretend
I do — here's what I can compare."* **Record the outcome** (listed / quoted /
flagged) — this is the moat data.

### 4.3 "I can't afford anything."
*Severity: P1, sometimes P0.* Move to options and rights, gently, without
financial advice.

> "There are real, dignified options at much lower cost than a traditional
> funeral, and you have rights here. Direct cremation and immediate burial are
> the lowest-cost paths; a home must list them on its price list and can't force you into a package to get them. You can also buy only
> what you want — no one can make you take a package. Let me show you the
> lowest-cost routes near you, and I'll point you to benefits you may qualify for
> (VA, Social Security, state/county indigent assistance)."

Boundaries: we **inform** about benefits and low-cost options; we **don't** advise
which assets to use, and we **don't** front or lend money (we never own the
funeral or its capital risk — guardrail #5). Hand off benefits-eligibility
specifics to the right office.

### 4.4 "I need this done today."
*Severity: P0.* Calm, concrete, immediate.

> "Okay — we can move quickly. Tell me the city and I'll pull the nearest options
> right now and what each needs from you to start today. Direct cremation or
> immediate burial are the fastest paths. I'll stay with you while you decide."

Then act fast; keep the family choosing. If the moment is genuinely urgent and
human throughput is the bottleneck, the founder takes it directly by phone.

### 4.5 "Are you a funeral home? Are you selling something?"
*Severity: P2.* Answer cleanly — this is a trust moment, not a deflection.

> "No — we're not a funeral home and we don't sell funerals or take any commission
> from one. We're a free, neutral guide that helps you see fair prices and your
> options. You never pay us."

### 4.6 "Do you get paid by them? What's the catch?"
*Severity: P2.* This is the brand in one answer. Be direct and a little proud.

> "Fair question. We take **zero** money from funeral homes or insurers — ever.
> We're paid by institutions like hospices to give the families they serve a
> neutral guide. That's why we can show you the real picture: no one who profits
> from the price is paying us. If we ever steered you toward a home for money,
> the whole thing would be worthless — so we don't."

### 4.7 "Which home should I pick?" (the steering trap)
*Severity: P1.* The most important "no" we say.

> "I won't pick for you — and that's on purpose. If I steered you, you couldn't
> trust me, and we'd be just another paid referral. What I'll do is put the
> options side by side — price, what's included, distance — so the choice is
> clear and it's yours."

### 4.8 Acute distress / crisis signals.
*Severity: P0.* If a family expresses thoughts of self-harm or is in crisis, we
are not a clinical service. Stay present, don't counsel.

> "I'm really glad you told me. I'm here, but I'm not the right kind of help for
> that part, and you deserve someone who is. In the US you can call or text 988
> any time — they're good people and they'll stay with you. I'm not going
> anywhere on the funeral side whenever you're ready."

Provide **988** (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline). Then, gently, the practical help
remains available. Log that a referral was made; do not record clinical detail.

## 5. Resource handoffs (where "not us" goes)

| Need | Hand off to | We do |
|---|---|---|
| Mental-health crisis | **988** Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call/text) | Warm referral, stay available |
| Grief counseling | Their **hospice's bereavement program** (13-month CoP mandate) or GriefShare / local groups | Point + encourage |
| Legal questions (estate, disputes, contracts) | A licensed attorney; legal aid if low-income | Explain rights factually, no advice |
| Financial / estate decisions | Their financial advisor / estate attorney | Explain costs + benefits, no advice |
| Benefits eligibility | **VA** (burial benefits), **SSA** (lump-sum death payment), county indigent-burial office | Explain what exists, help them find the office |
| A complaint about a home | State funeral board / FTC Funeral Rule complaint | Help document; advocate |

## 6. Escalation path

1. **Frontline** (founder, or future contractor) resolves P1/P2 directly.
2. **Anything touching a guardrail** — steering pressure, a money-from-a-home
   question, a savings/price claim we're unsure we can defend, PHI we shouldn't
   hold — pauses and goes to the **founder** before we answer.
3. **Legal / compliance exposure** (a steering accusation, a regulator, a data
   concern, a threatened dispute) → **founder → counsel.** Do not improvise a
   legal position. Route to `legal@honestfuneral.co` and stop.
4. **Anything that could harm the trust brand** is treated as urgent regardless
   of dollar size. One exposed exaggeration undoes the brand (bible Part 7).

## 8. Consent & follow-up (CAN-SPAM / privacy note)

- **Support replies are fine** — answering a family who contacted us is expected
  and not marketing.
- **Any follow-up that is promotional or a newsletter requires consent.** Don't
  add a support contact to a marketing list because they called us. Separate
  "needs an answer" from "wants ongoing email."
- **Every commercial email** carries a real physical mailing address and a
  working one-click unsubscribe, and we honor opt-outs promptly (CAN-SPAM).
- **Collect the minimum.** No PHI, no cause of death, nothing we don't need for
  the case (HIPAA minimization; families self-enroll). If a family sends health
  detail unprompted, don't propagate it into records.
- **Outcomes data is de-identified** for the dataset and the hospice report; the
  family's identity stays owner-scoped and private (RLS).
- **On request, we delete.** If a family asks us to delete their data, we do, and
  confirm it. See [`PRIVACY_RETENTION.md`](PRIVACY_RETENTION.md).

## 9. After every meaningful contact (the data reflex)

Because the family service exists to produce the moat, close each real case by
capturing what we can defend — in `/admin/outcomes`:

- [ ] Listed / quoted / negotiated / paid figures (whatever we learned)
- [ ] Chosen home (their choice, recorded — not steered)
- [ ] Any hidden-fee or Funeral-Rule-violation incident
- [ ] A satisfaction read (how the family felt they were treated)
- [ ] Any resource referral made (988, VA, counsel) — without clinical detail

This is the proof sheet a hospice buys and the data nobody else has. The human
conversation and the dataset are the same act.

## 10. The tone, in one paragraph (keep this in your ear)

Calm, plain-spoken, grief-aware, never salesy, never morbid. No performed empathy
— don't tell a grieving person how sorry you are at length; show it by being
useful and unhurried. No urgency, no upsell, no jargon. You are the steady voice
on the other end that is genuinely on their side and beholden to no one but them.
If a sentence sounds like marketing or like a script, cut it.
