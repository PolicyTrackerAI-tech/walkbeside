## 1. Executive summary

**Honest Funeral is building the free, neutral source of truth for funeral
pricing in America** — and the advocate that helps grieving families act on it.
About **3 million Americans die each year** into a funeral market where identical
services vary **100–200%+ in the same city**, only **~18% of funeral homes post
prices online**, and the FTC still does not require them to. Every party that
claims to help the family is conflicted: comparison sites are paid by the homes;
insurer-backed concierges serve the insurer.

We take the one seat no one else can occupy: **paid by no one with a stake in the
funeral price.** Families use our tools and advocacy **for free.** Revenue comes
from the **institutions that serve the dying and are required to support the
bereaved — hospices first, then employers** (a B2B2C model). Insurers may
*distribute* our tools to their beneficiaries, but **never pay us** — taking
insurer money is the conflict that defines our competitors (e.g., Empathy), not
us. We compound a **proprietary outcomes dataset** (what families were quoted vs.
what they actually paid) and a **conflict-free brand** into a durable moat and an
eventual premium acquisition.

- **Mission:** no family overcharged, misled, or alone at the worst moment.
- **Model:** free to families; institutions pay (recurring B2B2C).
- **Moat:** proprietary outcomes data + a neutral brand that compounds.
- **Two lines we never cross:** never take funeral-home or insurer money; never
  charge the grieving family.
- **Status:** product largely built; full operating plan complete; **pre-revenue,
  pre-first-pilot.** Near-term goal: **first paying hospice within ~90 days.**
- **Funding:** bootstrapped to date; planned raise **after** 1–2 paid contracts +
  free-tool traction.

## 2. The problem

Funeral pricing is opaque, exploitative, and structurally durable:

- **Scale & permanence.** ~3.0M U.S. deaths/year, rising past **3.6M by 2037**.
- **Opacity.** Only ~18% of homes post prices online; the FTC Funeral Rule
  guarantees an itemized price list **on request** but does **not** require online
  disclosure.
- **Price dispersion.** Identical services vary **100–200%+** within one metro.
  Median funeral costs run roughly **$6,280 (cremation) to $8,300 (burial).**
- **A worsening trend.** Private-equity roll-ups acquire independents and raise
  prices post-acquisition.
- **The worst possible buyer.** At-need purchasing is **once-in-a-lifetime,
  grief-state, time-pressured, and zero-repeat** — buyers can't comparison shop,
  and the upsell environment is engineered.
- **Everyone "helping" is conflicted.** Comparison directories are paid by the
  homes; insurer-backed concierges serve the insurer; the funeral home is the
  seller. **The neutral seat is empty.**

## 3. The solution

A three-layer product. **Families get the first two free; institutions buy the
third (and the neutral service it wraps).**

1. **Free source of truth** — a fair-price lookup, an "is this quote fair?"
   checker (photograph a price list → we flag overcharges + FTC violations),
   plain-language guides, a glossary, and a quarterly **Fair-Price Index.**
2. **Instrumented family advocacy** — we invoke the family's FTC right to itemized
   quotes, gather them from vetted homes, and present neutral side-by-side
   options. The family always chooses; **we never steer.** Its second job is to
   capture **outcomes data.**
3. **Institutional product** — a partner portal + reporting dashboard a hospice
   uses to enroll/refer families to the neutral tool and to **prove they
   delivered** their bereavement obligation.

## 4. Why now

- **Regulatory pressure** on funeral price transparency is building; being the
  cited data standard ahead of any rule is a durable advantage.
- **PE consolidation** is raising prices and public frustration.
- **Generative discovery (AI search)** rewards a **citable, original data
  source** — exactly the asset we build; the first neutral funeral-price dataset
  becomes the answer AI assistants give.
- **The hospice wedge is funded by mandate, not budget:** Medicare requires
  **~13 months of bereavement support** after each patient death
  (**42 CFR 418.64**), unfunded, and it shows up in publicly reported **CAHPS**
  family-satisfaction scores. Hospices need this and can't resource it.
- **The graveyard proves the model:** Cake, Willing, Farewill, Tulip, Halolife
  and others died or were absorbed — most for **charging the grieving family** or
  depending on funeral-home money. The payer must be an aligned third party.

## 5. Market

- **TAM (the pain):** ~3.0M deaths/yr × a median ~$6.3–8.3K funeral ≈ a
  **~$20B+ annual U.S. funeral market** (derived from the cited figures).
- **SAM (our beachhead payer):** U.S. hospices — roughly **~1.8M hospice deaths/
  year** delivered through **~5,000 providers**, each carrying the unfunded
  13-month bereavement obligation. A per-facility SaaS across thousands of
  providers is a multi-hundred-million-dollar recurring opportunity before
  employers/insurers.
- **SOM (year 1):** independent/regional **Utah hospices** — a drivable, fast-
  deciding beachhead to land the first paid contracts and the first outcomes data.
- **Expansion:** employers + benefits brokers (the bereavement gap), then insurers
  (beneficiary goodwill) — as **distribution partners, never as our payer.**

## 6. Business model & unit economics

- **Free to family. Institutions pay.** Pricing hypothesis: **per-facility annual
  SaaS, tiered by patient census** (predictable MRR); to be validated in pilots.
- **Revenue lines, in order:** (1) institutional B2B2C recurring — the engine;
  (2) anonymized data/Index licensing to non-conflicted buyers (researchers,
  employers, regulators) — later, highest margin; (3) lean/free consumer — proof +
  data, not a revenue target. **Never:** funeral-home fees or insurer-as-payer.
- **Cost structure (lean):** infrastructure runs in the **tens of dollars/month**
  early; **~$2 per advocacy case** in AI + email. The dominant early cost is
  founder time — the company can operate a long time on very little.
- **Gross margin** is software-like once the institutional product carries the
  service.

## 7. The moat

Four reinforcing powers, all resting on one rule (**no funeral-home/insurer
money**):

1. **Counter-positioning** — no incumbent paid by homes or insurers can be the
   neutral arbiter without breaking its own model (a 3–5 year window).
2. **Trust / brand** — built over years of conflict-free behavior; for a
   for-profit, the **track record is the structure** (the Consumer Reports /
   FAIR Health model).
3. **Conflict-free embedding** — switching costs once we're inside hospice and
   employer workflows.
4. **Proprietary outcomes data** — the **price list is scrapeable; the outcomes
   layer is not.** What families were quoted vs. paid, what advocacy negotiated,
   which homes flex — it exists nowhere else and compounds with every case. This
   is what makes us the cited standard and what an acquirer can't replicate.

## 8. Go-to-market

- **Land:** the **first paying hospice** via a disciplined 8-stage process — list
  → cold outreach → discovery → pitch → **free 60-day pilot** → results review →
  paid annual contract → peer referrals. **Pitch anchor:** the unfunded 13-month
  mandate + CAHPS + staff hours saved; **the proof** that they delivered.
- **Deliver by hand first:** the founder personally runs early pilot cases — the
  data engine doesn't require the institutional product to exist yet, which keeps
  go-to-market cheap and the partner portal a *post-yes* build.
- **Expand:** land-and-expand within hospice networks, then the employer channel,
  then insurer distribution.
- **Reach (free, compounding):** SEO topic clusters already built (a head-term
  page, **87 metro pages**, a **63-term glossary**, 40+ guides), generative-engine
  optimization to be cited by AI, and a quarterly **Fair-Price Index** as a
  recurring press + backlink + AI-citation event.

## 14. Risks & mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| An insurer-paid player (e.g., Empathy) expands into funeral pricing | Go deeper on **neutral** price advocacy + data than an insurer-paid model can; own the public source of truth; if matched, become the data layer they plug into |
| Hospice procurement is slow | Independents first; **free pilots**; a parallel employer/EAP channel |
| Anti-steering / compliance misstep | Neutral-by-design product; counsel clearance per state **before** launch |
| Health-data breach | Minimize PHI (family **self-enrollment**), BAAs, security basics, SOC 2 as we scale — over-invest (a breach is existential to a trust brand) |
| Data isn't defensible | Focus on the **outcomes** layer, not the public price list; if thin, lean on trust + regulatory position |
| Discovery-channel volatility (SEO/AI) | Diversify across SEO + AI citations + **institutional distribution** + an owned email list |
| Solo-founder bandwidth | Ruthless focus on the three engines; the scorecard; contractors before hires |
| Neutrality-compromise temptation | The guardrails — never take home/insurer money (the one unrecoverable mistake) |

## 16. The exit

The base-case outcome in this category is **acquisition by an insurer, platform,
or incumbent.** We run the company pure while it's ours and build the two things
an acquirer cannot replicate — a **trusted neutral brand** and **proprietary
outcomes data** — and take the best offer when the timing is right.
