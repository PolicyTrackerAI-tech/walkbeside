# Hospice Discovery-Call Script — Honest Funeral

*Use this live on a booked discovery call with a hospice. Goal: confirm there's real pain, learn how they handle families today, surface the referral-reputation pressure, identify who signs, and end by proposing a free 60-day pilot. Talk less than half the time — every "listen for" cue is a thing to write down. Don't pitch features; ask, then mirror back what you heard.*

## 1. Warm open (60–90 seconds — set the tone, then get out of the way)

> "Hi [First name], thanks for making the time — I know your days are full. I'll keep this to about [20] minutes and it's all questions, not a pitch. I just want to understand how [Hospice] handles families after a death and see if there's a way we can take some weight off your team. Honest Funeral is a free, neutral guide that helps grieving families through funeral pricing and the after-death paperwork maze — and the reason I built it the way I did is that we take **no money from funeral homes or insurers**, so we're something you can actually hand a family without it being a referral. Mind if I ask you a few things about how your program works today?"

**Listen for:** whether they relax (good — keep it conversational) or stay guarded (slow down, ask more, pitch less). If they immediately ask "what does it cost us?" — park it warmly: *"I'll get there, and the honest answer is the pilot is free — let me understand your world first so I'm not wasting your time."*

---

## 2. Their bereavement program + who runs it

> **2.1** "Walk me through your bereavement program — who runs it day to day, and how do you cover the full window after a death?"

**Listen for:** is it one part-time chaplain or counselor stretched thin? A named coordinator? Volunteers? The thinner the staffing, the bigger your wedge. Note the name of whoever owns it — that's your likely champion.

> **2.2** "How do families actually engage with it — do most take you up on the support, or do a lot drift away after the service?"

**Listen for:** low engagement / "we mail things and don't hear back" = an opening. They're carrying a federal obligation (the ~13-month bereavement requirement under 42 CFR 418.64) they can't fully deliver against. Don't lecture them on the reg — just let them describe the gap.

> **2.3** "Where does keeping that program running stretch you thinnest — is it staffing, the volume right after a death, or the range of things families need?"

**Listen for:** "we're not resourced for X." Whatever X is, note it verbatim — you'll mirror it back at the pilot ask.

---

## 3. How they handle funeral cost + after-death admin today (the wedge)

> **3.1** "When a family is deciding on a funeral, or drowning in paperwork after the death — death certificates, closing accounts, benefits, the dozens of little tasks — who helps them with that right now?"

**Listen for:** "they're kind of on their own," "our social worker fields it," "the chaplain gets those calls." If a staff member is absorbing funeral-pricing and admin questions, that's unfunded time you can quantify in step 5.

> **3.2** "Do families come back to your team confused or upset about a funeral bill — feeling like they overpaid?"

**Listen for:** any story of a family that got overcharged. This is gold — it's the exact harm you remove, and it's the harm that damages *their* reputation by association. Capture the anecdote.

> **3.3** "Do you ever point families toward funeral homes, or do you stay out of it?"

**Listen for:** they almost certainly say they stay neutral / can't recommend. Affirm it: *"Exactly right — and that's the whole design of what we do. We never name a home; we lay out the options and the family chooses. It's built to satisfy anti-steering law, not skirt it. That's why a hospice can ethically hand it to a family."* This pre-loads the "is this steering?" objection so it never comes up.

---

## 4. Referral sources + reputation (lead their ROI here — this is the real census driver)

> **4.1** "Where do most of your referrals come from — hospitals, SNFs, physicians, families telling other families?"

**Listen for:** the names of their referral sources. Their census depends on those relationships and on word-of-mouth from families.

> **4.2** "When a family has a great experience all the way through — including after the death — how much does that come back to you as referrals or reputation with those sources?"

**Listen for:** any acknowledgment that family experience drives word-of-mouth and referral-source goodwill. This is your core value argument: *a family we help through the funeral and the admin maze is a family that speaks well of [Hospice] — to the next family, and to the discharge planners who feed you.* Let them say it; then reinforce it gently.

> **4.3** "Are your emotional-and-spiritual-support scores something leadership watches — does the marketing or community-liaison side feel pressure there?"

**Listen for:** who owns reputation/scores. *Note:* if they mention CAHPS, frame it as **reputation and public reporting** — never as "more Medicare money." (CAHPS is pay-for-reporting; overpromising reimbursement burns trust.) The point is: their scores and reputation are public, and a documented, supported family experience helps the story they tell referral sources.

---

## 5. The cost of the gap (quantify it — you'll use this at the close and in pricing)

> **5.1** "Roughly how many hours a week does your team spend on things *outside* grief counseling — funeral logistics, cost questions, after-death admin?"

**Listen for:** any number. Even "a few hours a week per counselor" × loaded hourly cost = the framing that makes a small annual fee look cheap. Write the number down.

> **5.2** "If that maze just… got handled, and you got a report proving every family was supported — what would that be worth to your team's time and to your families' experience?"

**Listen for:** them doing your value math for you. Don't fill the silence — let them assign the value.

---

## 6. Decision process, budget reality, and BAA / security stance

> **6.1** "If a free pilot proved this helped your families and saved your team time, who besides you would need to say yes?"

**Listen for:** the name of the **Executive Director / Administrator** — the economic buyer who signs. Also note whether **community-liaison/marketing** should be in the room (they own the referral-reputation argument). Get names and roles.

> **6.2** "When you bring on a new tool or partner, what does that path usually look like — and is there a budget cycle I should know about?"

**Listen for:** decision timeline and budget reality. Be honest with yourself: a paid contract is realistically **4–6 months out**, not 90 days. You're mapping the path, not closing today.

> **6.3** "One practical thing — because families would enroll themselves directly with us, we don't need to touch any of your patient health information. Does that match how your team thinks about privacy and HIPAA, or would your compliance folks still want a paperwork review?"

**Listen for:** relief that self-enrollment sidesteps PHI/HIPAA friction, or a request for documentation. If they want paper: *"Easy — I've got a simple BAA and a one-page security summary I can send so your compliance team has it on file."* Don't let security become the reason to stall; have the docs ready.

---

## 7. Transition to the pilot offer (the close)

Mirror back the two or three pains they named, then make the ask.

> "Here's what I'm hearing: [their staffing gap, in their words], families [drifting / on their own] with funeral costs and after-death paperwork, and you want every family's experience to come back to you as goodwill with [their referral sources]. That's exactly the gap we fill — the funeral-pricing and admin maze your counselors aren't resourced for — and we hand you a report that documents you delivered it across the bereavement window.
>
> What I'd love to do is a **free 60-day pilot**: you hand ~10–15 of your families a simple card, they enroll themselves — no PHI, no work for your team — and we run every case by hand. At the end I show you families served, satisfaction, average savings versus their original quote, and time-to-resolution, on **your own families**, so you're not taking my word for any of it. No cost, no commitment, and if it doesn't help your families we shake hands and you owe nothing. Can I send you the one-page outline and grab a time to walk it through?"

**Listen for:** "send me something" or "let me loop in [ED name]" = a yes-shaped answer — move to scheduling immediately. If they hesitate, ask: *"What would you need to see to feel good about a small free pilot?"* and address that specific thing.

---

## Objection quick-reference (if they come up mid-call)

- **"Is this steering / a kickback?"** → "No, and it can't be — we present neutral options, the family chooses, and we take zero funeral-home money. That's precisely why a hospice can ethically use us."
- **"We have no budget."** → "The pilot is completely free for 60 days. And we're exploring aging and bereavement grant funding that may help cover the ongoing cost, so budget shouldn't be the thing that decides this."
- **"We already do bereavement."** → "Your counselors do the grief work — we don't replace that. We handle the funeral-pricing and after-death admin maze they aren't resourced for, and we hand you the documented report."
- **"How do we know the prices are right?"** → "Published methodology and real itemized price-list data — and in the pilot you'll see it on your own families, not a brochure."

---

*Funnel reality check: aim for ~10 of these discovery calls to yield ~2–3 pilots and ~1–2 paid contracts. Better than 1-in-5 to paid is product-market fit. A pilot can be **signed** within ~90 days; the pilot itself **runs 60 days**; the first **paid** contract is realistically **4–6 months** out. Be honest about that timeline — with yourself and with them.*
